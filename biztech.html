Problem Statement

Single bedroom apartments are a cost effective method to house lots of people in an urban area, but they leave people feeling isolated and lonely.

3rd on Maslow's hierarchy of needs is the need for belonging, intimacy, and community.

"Maslow called the bottom four levels of the pyramid ‘deficiency needs’ because a person does not feel anything if they are met, but becomes anxious if they are not." -Psychology Today

Love and belonging are hence classified as deficiency needs. A person will feel anxious if this need is not fulfilled.

+

Loneliness is deadly. A study called "Loneliness and Social Isolation as Risk Factors for Mortality" found that living alone increased likelihood of mortality by 32%.

+

Loneliness is common. In a study with 1604 participants, 43% reported feeling lonely. "Vivek Murthy, the surgeon general of the United States, has said many times in recent years that the most prevalent health issue in the country is not cancer or heart disease or obesity. It is isolation." -The Boston Globe

∴

You'll likely die prematurely if you're deprived of social interaction, and it is very common to feel lonely.

Desk Research

Insight 1 // Opportunity areas

"Apartments make up almost 1/3 of housing stock in America’s largest cities, with half of all of them being home to only a single person."

"This approach to architecture is part of a larger cultural obsession with privacy — a concept that is very modern relative to human history, according to Christopher Leinberger, a senior fellow at the Brookings Institution."

"In the 20th century, for the first time in human history, we had the opportunity to build isolated developments in metropolitan areas, and we did it with a vengeance."

"Things like shared ethnicity and culture are powerful ways to create a community within a building that was never designed for one."

Why a City Block can be the Loneliest Place on Earth, Grist.org

Insight 2 // Opportunity areas

"Living in a city is an unnatural state for human beings. [We evolved] living in small groups of around 100 to 150 people" -Colin Ellard, cognitive neuroscientist at Canada’s University of Waterloo.

"Being in constant contact with strangers can lead to feelings of social isolation and loneliness, and then of course have mental health consequences."

"On the surface, this may seem paradoxical: Cities are full of people—an antidote to loneliness. But living anonymously alongside millions of others can actually leave us isolated. Case in point: London is the loneliest place in the UK, according to a survey by ComRes."

"With this in mind, making cities less stressful is crucial. For Ellard, this means policies that encourage people to cycle or walk, as well as noise bylaws and more green spaces. Better urban design may help mitigate loneliness, too."

All the Ways Living in a City Messes With Your Mental Health, Vice.com

Insight 3 // Opportunity areas

"The way cities are designed can also contribute to levels of loneliness, including the ever-growing trend of replacing public spaces with more blocks of unaffordable flats."

"The trick is to help people to feel a little bit more connected to strangers, and that can be done by generating positive moods in public places."

"Shared common areas may hold the answer – giving us more chance of connecting with others."

What about using technology to connect people? "Being in constant virtual contact with friends or family may feel sociable, but research suggests this isn’t necessarily the case. A US study published last year found those who spend over two hours a day on social media are more likely to feel socially isolated."

Why is Living in a Big City so Isolating?, CityMetric.com

Ethnographic Research

Insight 4 // Opportunity areas

Q: Why did you move out of the city?

A: It's like living in a fishbowl. You're trapped and you can only watch as things happen around you. I like going for walks and now I live closer to the ocean.

-Jeremy Taylor, UX design contractor

Jeremy probably wants more nature and open spaces to go for walks.

Insight 5 // Opportunity areas

"All my friends in Italy are jealous of my new life in Amsterdam. They assume everything must be great because I have a good job, my English is improving and I live in a great city. It sounds amazing from the outside, but it's not really true for me."

"Most people look forward to the weekend, but for me, it’s the other way around – I look forward to Monday so I can speak to people at work. I feel really down on Fridays, because I start thinking about how I’m going to spend another weekend alone, mainly cleaning my room and playing on my computer. When I do venture outside, it’s almost always just for the sake of it – I go to a bar or grab a quick coffee, then I go home and feel alone again. It’s the same every weekend"

-Mario, works at an architecture firm

Mario wants to meet other people, and visits local coffee shops in an attempt to do so.

Expert Interviews

Insight 6 // Opportunity areas

Architect Grace Kim thinks that the solution is cohousing.

Kim lives and works in a cohousing community she designed in Seattle, where families or individuals each have their own homes, but the space was designed for interaction.

In the common house, everyone dines together three times a week, taking turns cooking for each other. Kim only cooks once every six weeks; two other times, she helps with prep and cleanup.

"It turns out when you eat together, you start planning more activities together."

"When you eat together, you share more things. You start to watch each other’s kids. You lend each other power tools. You borrow each other’s cars."

"When I said earlier that cohousing is an antidote to isolation, what I should have said is that cohousing can save your life."

Opportunity Area 1: Physically bringing strangers together

  • Insight 1 shows that fostering community culture within a housing complex can combat the effects of isolating architecture.
  • Insight 5 shows how individuals can feel isolated in a city even though they're always surrounded by people. We often need a catalyst to connect with strangers.
  • Insight 6 shows how expert Grace Kim has escaped loneliness through communal housing.

Opportunity Area 2: Facilitating open and/or shared spaces

  • Insight 2 says to combat the stress of overcrowding, the solution is to make cities less stressful. Encourage people to cycle or walk with space and infrastructure.
  • Insight 3 says that people need to be present and interact with people in their communities, which can be done by building shared common areas.
  • Insight 4 shows that an average person moved out of the city for better access to nature.
